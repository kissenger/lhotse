Beginner's guide to snorkelling in Britain

Snorkelling is defined by the oxford english dictionary as “the sport or activity of swimming underwater with a snorkel”. In practice, snorkelling provides an easy first step to safety explore the fascinating marine environment around us with a minimum of basic kit, and in a way that is suitable for all the family. The use of a diving mask and snorkel allows you to see and breathe with your face in the water, opening up a hidden world of sea dwelling critters that are usually the domain of divers.

But snorkelling’s only for warmer climes, right? Wrong!

There are some great places to snorkel around Britain and the UK, and plenty of marine-life to see for those willing to brave the colder waters.

Britain has some of the largest tidal ranges in the world, and has some fabulous examples of tidal habitats to explore. In this article we will explore some of the reasons to give snorkelling a go in Britain.

Do I need training to snorkel in Britain?

The simple and accurate answer is, of course, no. Many, many people get hold of a mask and snorkel from beach side shops in their summer holidays and give it a go. There is nothing wrong with starting to snorkel in this way, providing the limits of your experience and of the basic equipment are understood. Perfectly fine to use this equipment to explore the many fascinating rock pools or shallow water reefs in sheltered bays.

However, while formal training is not a necessity, there are good reasons to seek qualifications to take your snorkelling further - you will learn about how to select the correct kit, to use proper technique, and crucially how to stay safe in and around the water. All of these skills will add to your enjoyment in the long run, and make you a happier, more accomplished snorkeller.

The main agency to provide snorkel training in Britain is BSAC - a club based organisation with volunteer based instructors; the training you will receive through BSAC will be cost effective, but the number of clubs offering snorkel training is limited. If there are no clubs near you, there are many local companies offering small group snorkel experiences and training around Britain. While you won’t get a recognised qualification with this approach, you probably don’t need one, and you’ll get a great personalised experience.

How do I start snorkelling?

To start with, you only need a snorkel and a mask or goggles to be able to enjoy the undersea environment. Simply submerging your face standing-depth water is enough to start to explore life under the waves. As you build more confidence, you can add fins (flippers) and start to explore in deeper water, ensuring you are always aware of your limits of experience, and progress slowly. However, when you have your whole body in the water, you’ll need to start to think about a wetsuit to manage the Britains colder water.

Is it safe to snorkel in Britain?

Like any outdoor activity, snorkelling in Britain is as safe as you make it. There are many risks involved in snorkelling, no matter where you are in the world. However, although accidents happen, most risks can be mitigated with sufficient experience and care.

In Britain we are lucky that we don’t need to worry about sharks - the worst you are likely to come across is a nasty sting from a jellyfish, or uncomfortable prickle from a sea urchin. The biggest risk in the UK is probably from tides and currents. Only experience can give you the knowledge to be safe, so extend yourself slowly.

Where should I snorkel in Britain?

Britain has no shortage of fantastic places to go snorkelling, but there are some sites that are more suited to finding abundant marine life than others.

In general look for rocky locations with lots of seaweeds - these will tend to provide the nutrients and natural safety to attract a broad range of marine life.

If you are new to snorkelling, we would recommend starting off by finding larger rock pools to explore with a snorkel (the term 'snorkpooling' has recently been coined to describe this activity), or the sheltered shallows of a cove or harbor.

Where should I snorkel in Britain?

Crabs. There are more than 50 species of crabs living in our coastal waters, and it’s likely that one of your first encounters will be with a crab. Beyond that, it depends where you are snorkelling, and how lucky you are! With a bit of luck you should see starfish, anemones, gobies, blennies (our favourite is the Tompot Blenny ), jellyfish, lots of seaweeds and wracks. With more time in the water, or if you are very lucky, you could see cuttlefish, scorpionfish, dogfish or even an octopus.
