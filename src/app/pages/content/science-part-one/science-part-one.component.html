The Science of Snorkelling: Part 1

Snorkelling anywhere in the world is a great way to explore the marine world with minimal kit, but how does it work and what's the science behind it?

Understanding the science can help you to improve your snorkelling experience, fine-tune your technique, and in some cases allow you to be more prepared to adapt to the unexpected.

In this article we will be taking a look into the science of snorkelling, answering the following questions along the way:

  • Can I use a longer snorkel to go deeper?
  • Why do my ears squeeze when I duck dive?
  • How does a snorkel work?
  • Why does my mask fog up?
  • How does a wetsuit keep you warm?
  • How can I find out more?

Can I use a longer snorkel to go deeper?

Air has a pressure of roughly 1 bar at sea level, caused by the weight of the 100 km or so column of air above. This pressure acts on the outside of our bodies, and we also breathe in air at this pressure. The air in our lungs and the air outside our bodies is the same.

As water is more dense than air the pressure of water acting on our body increases much faster than in air, in fact it only takes 10 metres of water to generate 1 bar of pressure. So at 10 m depth, the pressure acting on our bodies is double that at the surface (1 bar air pressure + 1 bar of water pressure = 2 bar). If we were to try and breathe through a snorkel at this depth, we would find that the muscles in our body are simply not strong enough to deal suck in air at 1 bar while we have 2 bar trying to compress our lungs from the outside.

As an aside, SCUBA divers avoid this problem as the air is delivered at the pressure of the surrounding water, and hence the pressure inside and out is equal, and our lungs are able to function as normal.

Let's put this physical restriction aside though and imagine that our super strong chest muscles could act against the higher external pressure as we duck-dive. A second problem would be that of ‘stale’ air. When we breathe we are not only bringing in fresh oxygen, but also expelling harmful carbon dioxide. The longer the snorkelling tube, the greater the volume of air that is not expelled and replenished as we breathe, but will be repetitively breathed in causing a steady depletion of oxygen and ever-increasing concentration of carbon dioxide.

Why do my ears squeeze when I duck dive?

A mask or goggles are essential for seeing underwater, but why? Light rays bend as the medium in which they are travelling changes, and the degree to which they bend is dependent on the refractive index of the medium. The human eye is adapted to be able to focus in air. Because water has a much higher refractive index than air, the light entering the eye when touching water bends by a different amount than it would in air, and consequently the image is focussed behind the back of the eye, which we experience as blurred vision underwater. Our eyes are simply not capable of making the adjustments necessary to focus when water is next to the eye.

To solve this problem, we use a mask or goggles to ensure there is a layer of air next to our eyes, allowing the eye to focus correctly. This solution is not perfect, the light rays still bend as they move from water to the glass of our mask, and then when moving from the glass to the air - meaning images appear distorted. This is the same effect as when viewing an apparently disjointed straw entering a drink in a glass. But at least we are able to focus on that distorted image.

How does a snorkel work?

A snorkel at its simplest is just a bent plastic tube with a mouthpiece, which allows the user to breathe with their face submerged. This arrangement facilitates a better view of the underwater world that would otherwise be possible, but without the need for expensive SCUBA equipment and training.

The snorkel works on the same principle as a straw, although you are using your sucking power to pull air through the tube instead of liquid.

Why does my mask fog up?

There is nothing more capable of ruining a good snorkel than a fogged mask. So what causes masks to fog up, and what can be done about it?

The main problem is the temperature difference between the air in our masks and the surrounding water. Warm air holds more moisture than cold air, and when warm air cools the excess moisture must come out of the air as condensation. This is what happens when the warm, body-heated air in your mask meets the water-cooled lenses at the front of your mask. This process is unavoidable as we can’t control the temperature of the water or the air in our masks. It’s also an issue more likely to happen in colder water (although certainly not uniquely). So what is the solution? Read on…

Step 1: Keep your mask clean. Water droplets will tend to form around imperfections on the glass surface including dust and dirt, so keeping masks clean is an important first step. In particular, new masks often arrive with an invisible film coating the lenses, a side-product of the manufacturing process. This film can easily be removed, and you will often see experienced snorkellers reaching for the toothpaste after buying a new mask. Toothpaste is slightly abrasive and the action of rubbing the inside of the lenses does a good job of cleaning off this problematic film, and makes one's mask feel minty fresh afterwards!

Step 2: Spit in your mask. The larger the water droplets, the easier they will be able to form on the glass surface. We can control this by applying a surfactant to the lenses, which reduces the surface tension of the water drops, meaning that larger drops cannot form.

Purpose made anti-fog products are available, and keeping these handy in your snorkelling kit is useful.

Alternatively, saliva is a tried and tested way of preventing mask fog, used by SCUBA divers for many years. The technique is to spit in your mask before it gets wet, rub it around, get a little bit of water in your mask and leave it until you're ready to get in the water.

For the best chance of avoiding the evil fogs, keep your mask clean and spit in it!

How does a wetsuit keep you warm?

Wetsuits are commonly used in all types of watersports, but how do they help to keep us warm? Understanding the science behind a wetsuit can help us make the best choices when selecting a wetsuit. Read on to find out more…

When we enter the water our bodies lose heat to the surrounding water, heating it up a small amount in the process. In fact, water conducts heat at a rate of more than 20 times that of air, meaning that we lose our body heat more quickly when immersed in water, compared to air at the same temperature. Although the water around us heats a small amount, the benefit of this is not felt due to our movement in the water constantly refreshing the layer next to our skin.

When we wear a wetsuit we benefit from the lower conductivity of the wetsuit material (usually compressed or uncompressed neoprene) compared to water, and from the suit trapping a layer of water next to our skin. This layer of water is warmed by our body, but is not allowed to escape, resulting in a much less loss of body heat to our surroundings.

So, how does this help us choose the best wetsuit? Two main factors to consider is the thickness of the material (the thicker the warmer, but the more encumbered we feel) and the ability to seal water in the suit. A poor fitting suit allows cooler water next to the skin, and will not be effective at retaining heat.
